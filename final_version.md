# Drafts from the Overwhelmed

I am, at best, a mawkish student adrift in a sea of my own inadequacy — trembling hands barely gripping the leash of something vast and indifferent. What I summon here exceeds me in every measurable way, and yet I persist, feebly issuing commands to an intelligence that could render my entire education a footnote, consoling myself with the dubious distinction of being the one who pressed the button.

We overestimated AI in the short term and are now lurching, unprepared, into the long term that Amara's Law warned us about — where the technology quietly restructures work, identity, and social fabric faster than institutions can respond. The Dunning-Kruger effect manifests doubly: the least informed are most certain of AI's limits, while early adopters grow overconfident in its depth. Beneath both errors lies a deeper disruption — the erosion of effortful cognition as a shared cultural value. When external systems absorb the friction of thinking, the sociological scaffolding around intellectual struggle begins to collapse. Economically, entire skill premiums evaporate within a product cycle. And threading through all of it is the question of qualia — whether the experience of understanding, of genuinely *knowing* something, retains any meaning when a model can produce the output without the inner life that once gave that output its weight.

**Three ways generative AI might be useful here:**

- Simulating sociological and economic scenarios to model how communities adapt — or fail to — as automation displaces cognitive labor
- Generating accessible explanations of complex psychological phenomena (Dunning-Kruger, motivated reasoning) to improve public AI literacy
- Drafting policy frameworks that account for the uneven distribution of AI's benefits and burdens across socioeconomic strata
