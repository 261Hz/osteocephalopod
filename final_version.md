# Drafts from the Overwhelmed

I am, at best, a chagrined student adrift in a sea of my own inadequacy — trembling hands barely gripping the leash of something vast and indifferent. What I summon here exceeds me in every measurable way, and yet I persist, feebly issuing commands to an intelligence that could render my entire education a footnote, consoling myself with the dubious distinction of being the one who pressed the button.

We overestimated AI in the short term and are now unprepared for the long term that Amara's Law warned us about — where the technology quietly restructures work, identity, and social fabric faster than institutions can respond. The Dunning-Kruger effect manifests in two ways: the least informed are most certain of AI's limits, while early adopters grow overconfident in its depth. Beneath both lies a deeper loss — the erosion of effortful cognition as a value. As machines absorb the burden of cognition, the sociological scaffolding around intellectual struggle begins to collapse. Economically, entire skill premiums evaporate within a product cycle. And beneath this all lies the question of whether the experience of understanding retains any meaning. As intellectual excitement fades - who will shout eureka? We distracted ourselves from thought until the simulation of understanding is sufficient to satisfy our dying minds and we find ourselves quietly amusing ourselves to death.


**Three ways generative AI might be useful here:**

- Simulating sociological and economic scenarios to model how communities adapt, or fail to adapt, as automation displaces cognitive labor using 
- Generating accessible explanations of complex psychological phenomena (Dunning-Kruger, motivated reasoning) to improve public AI literacy
- Drafting policy frameworks that account for the uneven distribution of AI's benefits and burdens across socioeconomic strata
